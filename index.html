Fundamentals · Section 01

History & Materials

Jewels have been used in watches since 1704, invented in the 1690s by Nicholas Facio. From garnet to synthetic corundum — the materials evolved dramatically over three centuries, each step reducing friction and improving reliability.

MOHS
Hardness Scale Comparison
7
Garnet
Earliest jewels. Softest — prone to wear. No longer used.
9
Ruby
Corundum (Al₂O₃). Standard for most modern jewels.
9
Sapphire
Same hardness as ruby — different trace impurity. Used interchangeably.
10
Diamond
Hardest known material. Cap jewels (endstones) only.
SYNTHETIC
Natural vs. Synthetic — Since the 1940s
Natural Stones (Pre-1940s)
Garnet, ruby, sapphire, and diamond from mines. Internal flaws are inherent in natural crystals — causing them to break more readily under stress than synthetic material.
Synthetic Corundum (Post-1940s)
Grown in controlled conditions — no internal flaws. More uniform in hardness and structure. Stronger than natural jewels. Used almost exclusively in modern watches.
Why Corundum?
Ruby and sapphire are both aluminium oxide — at Mohs 9, hard enough to resist steel pivot wear, yet not so brittle as to shatter under normal forces. The ideal bearing material.
Fundamentals · Section 02

The Purpose of Jewels

The main purpose of a jewel is to reduce friction in the balance and train — which reduces pivot wear, increases the life of the watch, and improves timekeeping performance.

FRICTION
The Physics of Pivot Friction

When a steel pivot rotates in a brass hole, two metal surfaces rub under constant load. This wastes power that should go to the balance wheel and wears both surfaces over time. Two factors control friction: pressure between surfaces (difficult to change — set by the movement's forces) and material pairing and surface finish. Replacing brass with polished hard jewels addresses both: the harder material resists grit embedding, and the polished surface dramatically reduces friction.

Grit resistance matters most at the centre wheel. Centre wheel pivots are under the highest pressure in the movement. Grit embeds in soft brass and acts like sandpaper on the pivot. In a jewel hole, grit cannot embed — the harder surface simply scrapes it away. Many brass centre wheel pivot holes become visibly grooved or almost completely worn through. When investigating low amplitude, the centre wheel pivots are a critical inspection point.

Every increment of friction reduction translates to amplitude. The first seven jewels are placed where the greatest gains occur — the balance and escapement. Each additional jewel above seven delivers diminishing but worthwhile returns.

Jewel Count by Grade

The number of jewels in a movement determines exactly where they are placed. There is a standard progression — each step adds jewels to the next most-beneficial position.

CountCumulative additionsPositions added
7 jewelsBase2 balance hole jewels · 2 cap jewels · 2 pallet stones · 1 roller (impulse) jewel. Greatest amplitude gain per jewel.
9 jewels+2One jewel per pivot of the escape wheel pinion.
11 jewels+2One jewel per pivot of the pallet arbor.
15 jewels+4Jewels on the third- and fourth-wheel pivots.
17 jewels+2Two jewels on the centre wheel pivots (preferred) or two cap jewels on escape wheel pivots. Centre wheel is the better choice — highest pressure point, most wear.
19 jewels+2Two cap jewels on the escape wheel pivots.
21 jewels+2Two cap jewels on the pallet arbor.
23 jewels+2The mainspring barrel jewelled with two jewels.
Exceptions exist. Some makers place cap jewels on the third- and fourth-wheel pivots and leave the centre wheel pivots in brass. The progression above is the most common pattern, not an absolute rule.

Jewel Types · Section 04

Bezel vs. Friction Jewels

Two fundamentally different jewel construction types have existed since roughly 1930. Understanding the difference is essential before any jewel replacement work.

Bezel-Set Jewels (Pre-1930s)
The jewel has a beveled corner — a thin metal bezel is rolled over it to retain it in the setting. Cannot be pressed into a plain hole. Setting this type made end shake adjustment impractical in many movements.

May be rubbed directly into the main plate, rubbed into a separate setting, or held by jewel screws.
Friction Jewels — "Rubbed In" (Post-1930)
The jewel has straight edges — no bevel. Pressed directly into a straight hole in the plate or bridge. Simpler end shake adjustment. Used almost exclusively since the 1930s.

Flexible: works in both a bezel setting and a plain friction hole. A bezel jewel can only do one.
⚠
A bezel jewel cannot be frictioned into a plain hole. The beveled corner will not pass cleanly into a straight bore. A friction jewel, however, can serve either role. When replacing a jewel, identify the original type and match it.
Jewel Types · Section 05

Cap Jewel Assemblies

The cap jewel (endstone) and balance hole jewel work as a pair. Their surface shapes determine how the oil ring forms — and whether the pivot will be properly lubricated over time.

OIL RING
Three Assembly Types
Flat + Flat
Older / lower-grade. Surfaces must be perfectly parallel — any tilt causes off-centre oil ring and dry pivot.
Flat + Convex
Better grade. Convex shape draws oil to centre above the hole — more reliable ring. Common modern configuration.
Convex + Convex
Highest grade. Both surfaces convex — closest point always above the pivot hole. Most reliably centred oil ring.
Best configuration: convex cap jewel facing convex balance hole jewel. The source states explicitly: "When both the cap jewel and the balance hole jewel have convex surfaces, it further enhances the performance of the assembly." Found only in the finest movements.

Capillary attraction holds the oil between the two jewels. If the surfaces are not parallel (flat-on-flat assembly), the oil is attracted toward the closest space — causing an off-centre ring that leaves the pivot dry on one arc of its rotation.

Jewel Types · Section 06

Olive-Hole & Convex Jewels

Higher-grade balance hole jewels feature an olive-shaped bore — convex on the inside surface — that reduces contact area in both watch positions and equalises resistance.

The olive-shaped hole is convex inside the bore. This reduces the contact area between the pivot and the jewel when the balance staff is horizontal — less friction than a plain cylindrical bore. At the same time, it minimises sidewall resistance when the watch is vertical. The result: resistance in horizontal and vertical positions is more nearly equal — critical for consistent timekeeping across all positions.

Horizontal
Pivot end rests on the cap jewel. The olive bore reduces sidewall contact — pivot spins primarily on its tip against the endstone.
Vertical
Pivot hangs in the jewel hole by its shoulder. Olive shape reduces this sidewall friction to approach the horizontal level.
Result
Equalised resistance means consistent amplitude in all positions — the key requirement for a watch adjusted to positions.
Replacement · Section 07

Removing Old Jewels

Two removal methods exist — one for shattered jewels, one for cracked but intact jewels. The condition of the jewel determines the approach.

A
Jewel Badly Crushed or Shattered
Break it out piece by piece using a needle or pegwood sharpened to a point. Work from the opposite side of the bezel — this is the easier direction to free fragments. Do not damage the bezel during removal.
B
Jewel Cracked or Chipped but Still Intact
A staking tool could push it out, but a friction jewelling tool gives more control and is preferred. Select a flat stump with a hole large enough for the jewel to clear. The pusher should be slightly smaller than the jewel.

The bezel is typically on the flat side of the jewel — push from the opposite (convex) side.
⚠
Protect the bezel at all costs. The thin metal bezel is extremely delicate. Once distorted or torn, it may be impossible to re-close correctly. All removal work must prioritise preserving the bezel for re-use with the new jewel.
Replacement · Section 08

Opening the Bezel

Think of the bezel as a thin metal flap that was folded down to retain the jewel. It must be eased open again — incrementally and with great patience.

1
Check for Fragments First
Before inserting the opening tool, clear any remaining jewel fragments from under the bezel. Even tiny shards will damage the tool jaws when they spread against the fragment. Inspect under magnification.
2
Insert the Bezel Opening Tool
Place the jaws inside the bezel groove. Spread slightly, then turn the tool in ¼-turn increments — not 360° rotations, which distort the bezel.
3
Incrementally Spread and Turn
Spread the jaws a small amount more, then turn again. Repeat until fully open. The bezel is extremely delicate — patience is not optional.
4
Inspect Under Magnification
The bezel must be visibly open all the way around. The new jewel should drop into the setting under its own weight without any force before proceeding to close.
Replacement · Section 09

Sizing the New Jewel

Three measurements determine jewel selection: hole size, outside diameter, and thickness. An error in any one causes a problem that only appears once the movement is running.

1
Hole Size — +0.02mm Over Pivot
Measure the pivot with a digital micrometer. Select a jewel with a hole 0.02mm larger than the pivot measurement.

Verification without micrometer: place jewel over pivot to the shoulder, then press on the jewel edge. The correct clearance produces a tilt of approximately 5°. More than this: hole is too large. Less: too tight.
2
Outside Diameter — No Sideshake
The outer diameter must fit the setting with no sideshake. A loose jewel will shift over time, tilting relative to the cap jewel and disrupting the oil ring.
3
Thickness — End Shake and Flush Seating
Thickness controls: pivot length in the hole, correct end shake on the pivot, and the jewel sitting flush with the setting or plate surface. Too thick: pivot binds. Too thin: excessive end shake and oil ring gap too wide — oil cannot bridge the gap.
Unknown jewel sizes: Use plug gauges or known pivots to determine hole sizes in an assortment. Drop a pivot of known diameter into the jewel — correct fit goes to the shoulder with approximately 5° tilt.
Replacement · Section 10

Closing the Bezel

With the new jewel seated, the bezel must be folded back down evenly. Two methods work — a dedicated bezel closer, or a polished needle in a pin vise.

A
Method 1 — Bezel Closing Tool
Select the end-piece matching the diameter of the bezel groove. Place in the groove. Turn in small ¼-turn increments. After each pass, adjust the tool slightly smaller and turn again. Repeat until the bezel is fully crowded over the jewel edge with no sideshake.

A light film of watch oil on the tool tip helps it move smoothly without catching.
B
Method 2 — Polished Needle in Pin Vise
Shape a needle to fit the bezel groove and highly polish the tip. Hold upright, place in the groove, and move in a circular motion around the jewel, gradually folding the bezel over.

Works when no dedicated closer is available — but only if the old jewel was pushed out cleanly (so the bezel was already partially opened by pressure).
Verify the result. No sideshake. Flush with the setting surface. Bezel folded evenly all the way around. Then verify pivot fit — drop to the shoulder, tilt approximately 5° under edge pressure.
Faults · Section 11

Out-of-Parallel Assemblies

If the cap jewel and balance hole jewel are not parallel, the oil ring will be off-centre — part of the pivot's rotation runs dry.

Cause 1 — Dirt in Setting
Any debris left in the jewel setting after cleaning holds one edge of the cap jewel up — tilting it relative to the balance hole jewel below. The setting appears correctly installed but the surfaces are not parallel.
Cause 2 — Stripped Screw
When the endstone setting is held by two screws, a stripped screw won't pull the setting fully down on one end. The setting rocks slightly, the cap jewel tilts, the oil ring shifts.
Amplitude Symptom
The two horizontal positions show very different amplitudes from each other. One pivot spins correctly on its tip; the other has more of its cone contacting the tilted endstone. This dial-up / dial-down asymmetry is a strong diagnostic signal.
Oil Ring Symptom
When oiling, the oil pulls to one side of the endstone instead of forming a centred ring. The oil follows the closest space between the two non-parallel surfaces — pulling to the edge rather than sitting over the pivot hole.
Fix for flat-on-flat assemblies: Replace one jewel with a convex surface — this eliminates dependence on perfect parallelism. The oil is then attracted to the closest point (above the hole, not to one side). Alternatively trace and eliminate the cause of tilt: clean the setting thoroughly, replace any stripped screws.
Faults · Section 12

Oil Space Faults

Too much gap and the oil cannot be pushed through. Too little gap and the ring is too large, then breaks away. Both extremes leave the pivot dry.

Too Much Space
Cause: Setting not fully seated — endstone too high.

Oil cannot be pushed through. If forced, it takes too much oil to form any ring — that excess breaks away and runs down the cone of the balance staff. Pivot runs dry.
Jewels Touching
Cause: Wrong jewel thickness — cap jewel too thick.

Oil cannot pass at all, or forms an oversized ring that immediately breaks away into the jewel groove. Usually result of incorrect replacement jewel thickness.
Correct Assembly
Oil ring is concentric and correct diameter. Gap is correct — oil pushed through normally, stable ring centred on the pivot hole. Will last the required service interval given clean jewels and quality oil.
Faults · Section 13

Over-Oiling Consequences

An off-centre ring tempts the watchmaker to add more oil. This creates a worse problem than the original fault.

1
The Temptation
When the oil ring is off-centre, the far edge may not reach the pivot hole. The natural response is to add oil until coverage is achieved. This is incorrect.
2
What Actually Happens
The outer edge of an oversized ring breaks away and runs into the groove around the jewel. Once in the groove, it no longer contacts the pivot. The pivot runs completely dry shortly after.
3
Oil Migrates to the Balance Spring
Oil in the groove around the jewel wicks up the balance spring. Oil on the spring changes its elasticity, disrupts isochronism, and causes rate variation. This is extremely difficult to correct without a complete service.
⚠
The correct response to an off-centre oil ring is to fix its cause — not add more oil. Identify why the jewels are not parallel (dirt, stripped screw, wrong jewel type) and correct it. Only then oil the corrected assembly with the correct quantity.
Settings · Section 14

Cap Jewel Setting Styles

Settings for cap jewels varied significantly between American, European, and English watches. Correct identification is essential for reassembly.

AMERICAN
Three Common American Styles
View A — Most Common
Counterbored for two screwheads. Sits flush with the balance cock or plate. Used for balance staff on all grades; also for escape wheel and pallet arbor pivots on high-grade watches. Brass (average) or gold alloy (high grade).
View B — Extended Type
Ledge sits flush with the plate surface; the screwheads tighten against this ledge rather than countersinks. The top of the setting stands above flush with the plate.
View C — Extended, Gold Alloy
Very high-grade watches only. Gold alloy. Counterbored for two screwheads. Found in Howard watches and some Walthams. Similar styles appear in some English and Swiss watches.
EUROPEAN
European Multi-Part Assembly

The European-origin style is a multi-part assembly. The cap jewel rests on top of the balance cock — not bezeled into a setting. A cap piece fits into the regulator's hole; its beveled hole presses the cap jewel down against the cock. Two cap screws pass through the cock from below and screw into the cap. Two requirements: the cap must press the jewel tight enough that it cannot rock, AND allow the regulator to be moved with the correct friction.

LOWER & UPPER
Other Setting Styles

Lower cap jewel settings (under the dial) come in three shapes: figure-eight, part figure-eight/part round, and fully round — each fitting a matching recess in the plate. Usually brass or steel. Upper settings (escape wheel and pallet fork) are gold alloy in high-grade watches, brass or steel in lower grades.

Settings · Section 15

Diamond Cap Jewels

Diamond endstones were used in marine chronometers and the finest American and English lever watches — where a pivot rests on the cap jewel nearly continuously.

Ruby and sapphire (Mohs 9) can eventually wear at the contact point where the pivot tip rests. Diamond (Mohs 10) resists this wear almost completely. The advantage is greatest where the pivot rests on the cap jewel all the time — as in a marine chronometer maintaining a face-up orientation at sea for months. For a pocket watch, the equivalent is overnight: placed on its back, the upper pivot rests on the upper cap jewel until morning.

Marine Chronometers
Primary application. Face-up at sea almost continuously — the upper balance pivot rests on the cap jewel without interruption. Diamond's resistance to tip wear was essential for long-term accuracy on extended voyages.
Waltham Premier
The Waltham Watch Company produced the Waltham Premier — an exceptional high-grade pocket watch featuring three pairs of diamond cap jewels. A pinnacle of American watchmaking.
English Lever Watches
Diamond cap jewels were used on the balance cock of many fine old English lever watches. Typically only the upper pivot received the diamond endstone — the position most subject to constant load during wearing.
Special Types · Section 16

English Lever & Verge Arrangements

Two earlier systems that predate modern screw-retained settings: the dovetailed English lever assembly and the verge watch bearing arrangements.

ENGLISH LEVER
Dovetailed Bar Assembly

In the English full-plate lever watch, balance hole and cap jewels are not held with screws. A dovetailed bar containing the cap jewel slides across a matching dovetail in the balance hole jewel setting. The complete assembly is fastened to the underside of the upper plate. A clever and elegant system requiring no screws in the traditional sense.

VERGE
French and English Verge Arrangements
French Verge
The cap for the lower staff pivot is made from hardened steel. The pivot works in a hole in the assembly with the steel cap over the pivot end.
English Verge
A brass plug set into the potence. Drilled only partway through — a blind hole — with the pivot working inside it. The blind bottom acts as the cap. Bottom smoothed to reduce resistance. Hard brass used to minimise wear in the bore.
The English verge blind hole is essentially a brass cap jewel substitute. Hard brass partially addresses the wear problem — but cannot match the wear resistance of corundum or diamond. The transition to jewelled bearings was a dramatic improvement from this earlier arrangement.
